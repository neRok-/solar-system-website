Intro

Of all the planets in our solar system, Pluto is the smallest. It is significantly smaller than the Earth’s moon. An American astronomer, Clyde W. Tombaugh, first found it in 1930, therefore making it the most recently discovered planet. The orbit of Pluto is very much elliptical, and for the equivalent of 20 years out of every 248, its orbit is closer to the Sun then Neptune’s orbit. Generally, though, it’s true to say that Pluto is the farthest known planet in our solar system. On Pluto it is cold, it is dark, and the Sun only appears as a bright star, causing the effect of a continuous night.

Composition

It is common belief that Pluto has a rock core, which is covered in ice. Its atmosphere is made up of methane, which freezes as it moves further and further away from the Sun.

Moons

Pluto has one moon, known as Charon, which was found by another American astronomer, James Christy, on 22nd June 1978.

It is believed that Charon once had the same, or a very similar composition to that of Pluto, but now Charon has frozen water, as opposed to Pluto’s frozen methane. It is possible that molecules of methane left the moon and travelled to Pluto due to its stronger gravitational pull.

As with all orbital systems, Pluto and Charon orbit around a centre of mass. This centre of mass is just of Pluto’s surface. 12 percent of this system’s mass is made up of Charon.

Charon’s diameter is approximately half that of Pluto’s. 

Pluto’s Orbit

Pluto’s orbit is, as earlier stated, elongated. It is inclined by 17 degrees to the plane created by Earth’s orbit (the plane of ecliptic). This is the greatest inclination of any of the planets’ orbits, and causes Pluto to be, at one point, 1.25 billion kilometres away from the corresponding point on the ecliptic plane.
