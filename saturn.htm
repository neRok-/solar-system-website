Intro

Saturn is the sixth planet from the sun, it is both the second gas giant, and also the second largest planet. Ice covered rocks form rings around Saturn, and three of these are visible through a telescope. A further four have been identified. It has been put forward that these rings were, or were going to be another moon.

Composition

Saturn has a rocky centre, covered by all the states of hydrogen (solid, liquid and gas), and also helium gas. Its density is less than all the planets, and less than water.

Atmosphere

Saturn exceedingly fast rotation causes hazy clouds to follow a path parallel to the equator. Saturn bulges around the equator, as do all the gas giants, which can also be attributed to the speed of rotation.

Saturn’s winds are counter flowing.

Ring System

As is stated, there are a total of seven rings around Saturn. These rings vary in composition from icy dust particles (mainly found further from the planet), and ice covered rocks that can reach lengths of 1 km. Between rings A and B, there is what has come to be known as the Cassini Division, a void spanning 4, 200 km. “Mimas”, one of the moons of Saturn, acts as a vacuum, with its gravitational forces sucking in almost all particles that venture into the division. The division does however, have “ringlets”.

The radius of Saturn’s main rings is 274,000. The rings form the thinnest known disk in the universe. A pancake with the same proportions would be 5mm thick, yet 1.4 km across. All you would have to do is a find a frying pan that big.

Moons

There are at least 20 moons in orbit of Saturn, which is the greatest number of moons that any planet has in our solar system. 13 were discovered from without the aid of space probes, while the other 7 were discovered through their use. The smallest of them are potato shaped like that of Mars.

Mimas, one of the smaller moons, has a crater that’s diameter is one third of the size of the moons. It is not unusual for Saturn’s moons to have craters.

Titan, the largest of Saturn’s moon and second largest in the solar system, is one of five moons that have an atmosphere. Hydrogen cyanide, one of the fundamental organic “building blocks” of evolution on earth, is present there, so it has been suggested that Titan, therefore, could support life in a number of years. A very LARGE number of years. However, it has been well noted that “Titan” has a temperature that seems unsuited to any form of life.

It has been noted that on Enceladus, there are broad region were no craters are present. This shows that there has been some form of geological activity “recently”.

Two moons, “Dione”, and “Rhea” have bright cratered leading hemispheres, and dark, almost crater free trailing hemispheres, however, Iapetus is quite the opposite, with a darker leading hemishere.
