Intro

Jupiter is the first planet after the asteroid belt, the first of the four gas giants, and the first largest planet in our solar system. It is also fifth from the Sun. Its volume is a whopping 1,300 times that of the Earth’s, and when you add the amount of mass that makes up all the other planets, it is two fifths of Jupiter’s mass. The diameter of Jupiter is eleven times Earth’s. Jupiter spins rapidly, with one Jupiter “day” being less than half an Earth day.

Composition

Jupiter has a core with temperatures exceeding 35,000 degrees Celsius. That core is surrounded by hydrogen under great pressure, which takes a solid form. Above that is hydrogen in its liquid form, and then helium

Atmosphere

The greatest part of the banded clouds of Jupiter are made up of helium and hydrogen gases. The red-brown and yellow-ochre clouds are made of ammonia crystals and solid methane respectively.

Jupiter’s atmosphere meets the surface about 1,000 kilometres below the clouds. The bands of clouds, called zones and belts, are produced by Jupiter's rapid spin, and these run parallel to the equator.

Spots, ovals and streaks have been known to last for years.

The Great Red Spot

The existence of the Great Red Spot has been known for at leats 300 years. It is an anticyclone like that of the Great and Small Dark Spots of Neptune. Its diameter is believed to be three times the Earth’s diameter. It completes one rotation of Jupiter every 12 Earth days, and it has been suggested that the  colour of the spot may be caused by the movement of phosphorous into the spot from the warmer regions that surround it.

Ring System

Jupiter has three rings: The “Halo ring” which is 22,800 kilometres wide; the “Main Ring”, which is 6,400 kilometres wide; the “Gossamer ring” which is 85,000 kilometres wide, but also the least dense. In 1979 the Voyager I discovered these rings.

Moons

Jupiter has sixteen moons. They can be divided into four groups, based on their distance from Jupiter. The first group is approximately 130,000 kilometres from Jupiter, with the second a further 200,000 kilometres, then the third and fourth 9,000,000 kilometres away, and about that distance again.

All the moons orbit with Jupiter’s direction of rotation with the fourth, outermost, group the exception. The second group, which has come to be known as the Galilean group, is the only group which doesn’t have small moons. These moons are: Ganymede, Io, Callisto and Europa. These four were discovered by Galileo in 1610, and have been named accordingly, and can be seen through a small telescope.
