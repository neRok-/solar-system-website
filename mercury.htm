  Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun and is the second smallest planet in the Solar System. Because it must race around the Sun faster than any other planet in order to maintain a stable orbit, Mercury has the shortest year of any planet, taking just 88 Earth days to orbit the Sun once, at an average speed of about 48 km per second. During each year, Mercury spins very slowly one and one half times on its axis. This produces a long sidereal day lasting almost 59 Earth days. Mercury’s solar day, which is measured from sunrise to sunrise, lasts for 176 Earth days. Mercury’s solar day is thus twice as long as its year.

Being so close to the Sun, with no atmosphere to keep heat in during the night, Mercury has a surface temperature that ranges from -180 °C to 430 °C .

Composition

Mercury has a high density, with about 80 per cent of its mass in its huge core, composed of iron and nickel. The core is some 1800 km in radius and is surrounded by a rocky mantle and crust that together are about 600 km in thickness.

Surface Features

Mercury’s surface is scarred by thousands of craters and by scarps (cliffs) that formed when the planet’s young core cooled and shrank, contracting the surface crust. Most of the craters formed around 3.5 billion years ago when the planet was bombarded by meteorites. Mercury’s craters range in size from a few metres to more than 1,000 km across. The largest are called basins. Crater characteristics include mountainous peaks and rings, terraced walls, and rays of bright ejecta (material thrown out by the impact). A crater’s characteristics depend on the size, speed, and direction of the impacting meteorite. The Caloris Basin, at 1,300 km across, is the largest impact basin to be discovered on Mercury. It is surrounded by concentric rings of mountains thrown up by an enormous impact. The floor of the basin has been largely filled with solidified lava, and is heavily marked by smaller, younger craters. It was called “Caloris” from the Latin word for “heat”,, because on each alternate occasion that Mercury approaches closest to the Sun, the basin happens to point towards the Sun and is the hottest place on the planet.

Atmosphere

Mercury’s surface gravity is too weak to hold on to gas particles near the surface. As a result, Mercury’s atmosphere is virtually nonexistent, being about 1,000 billion times less dense than the Earth’s atmosphere. Only small amounts of helium and hydrogen gases are present.
