Venus, the second planet from the Sun, is a rocky sphere blanketed by dense, yellowish clouds. It is the Earth’s nearest planetary neighbor, and is similar to the Earth in size and density. Its thick atmosphere strongly reflects sunlight, making Venus the brightest object in the sky after the Sun and Moon. Any sunlight and heat that manage to penetrate Venus’s clouds remain trapped close to the planet’s surface, making it intensely hot. Venus is the hottest planet in the Solar System.

Venus is the slowest spinning of all the planets, taking 243 Earth days to rotate once. When viewed from above the North Pole, most planets and moons in the Solar System rotate on their axes and orbit the Sun in an anticlockwise direction. Venus, however, rotates in a clockwise, or retrograde, direction in comparison with the other planets. Why it does this is not known for certain, but some astronomers have suggested that Venus may have spun in the same direction as the other planets before it was knocked upside down by a collision with another planet or an asteroid.

Composition

Venus’s core, consisting of semisolid iron and nickel, is about 3,000 km in radius. The core is surrounded by a rocky mantle, about 3,000 km thick, and a 50-km thick outer crust composed of silicate rocks.

Surface Features

Spacecraft have mapped 98 per cent of Venus’s surface using radar. Overall, the surface is much flatter than the Earth’s, and huge volcanic plains of hot desert cover about two-thirds of the planet. Several plateau regions rise a few kilometres above the plains of Venus. At more than 11 km above average ground level, the mountainous region of Maxwell Montes is the highest point on Venus. Volcanoes are scattered over the entire surface, some measuring more than 160 km across.

Atmosphere

Venus’s thick atmosphere makes for a hot, suffocating world: surface temperatures reach 480°C and the atmospheric pressure is 90 times that on Earth. The atmosphere consists mainly of carbon dioxide with some nitrogen and traces of carbon monoxide, sulphur dioxide, and water vapour. The clouds’ yellow colour is caused by sulphuric acid. The levels of this acid vary, which suggests that some volcanoes on Venus may still be active. On both Venus and the Earth, shorter-wave infrared radiation from the Sun passes through the atmosphere, heats the ground, and is given back out as longer-wave infrared radiation. On the Earth, some of this infrared radiation, or heat, is trapped in the atmosphere, causing the “greenhouse effect”. On Venus the greenhouse effect is complete, as all the heat is trapped in the atmosphere.

Terraforming

The process of making other planets more Earthlike is called terraforming. Some scientists have suggested that this process could be started on Venus by “seeding” the atmosphere with plant spores. Doubts have been raised over whether any life from Earth would survive in Venusian temperatures, but in theory, the spores would absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and release oxygen by photosynthesis. This would set in motion a chain of events that might lead to conditions more suitable for supporting life as we know it.
