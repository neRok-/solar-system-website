Asteroids

 

More than 90 per cent of asteroids in the Solar System are in the asteroid belt. These follow orbits around the Sun that take between 3 and 6 years to complete.

Asteroids may be carbonaceous, stony, or metallic. Scientists think the asteroids would probably have stuck together to form a planet had they not been prevented from doing so by Jupiter’s powerful gravitational field. The total mass of all the asteroids combined is probably less than one-thousandth that of the Earth or one-twentieth that of the Moon. In celestial terms, asteroids are very small objects.

Ceres, at 933 km across, is the largest-known asteroid and the first to have been discovered, in 1801. It is an unusually large asteroid. Most asteroids are no larger than 250 km across. It has been estimated that nearly 1 billion asteroids are wider than 1 km. Those with a diameter greater than about 300 km are spherical. Smaller asteroids are irregularly shaped. The smallest asteroids are specks of dust, no greater in diameter than a pinhead.

Asteroids are numbered according to the order in which their orbits became known. The larger asteroids are also named, usually by their discoverer. The rest are generally classified in groups, and each group follows a different orbit. The Trojans are two collections of exceptionally dark asteroids that follow Jupiter’s orbit. One subgroup orbits ahead of Jupiter, the other behind. The Amor, Apollo, and Aten groups contain asteroids that orbit closer to Earth.

Originally names were taken from mythology and were usually feminine (for example Vesta, Pallas, and Juno). As mythological sources were exhausted, names of states, cities, colleges, composers, writers, astronauts, and so on, were used. Today anything goes with asteroid names: there is even one, Asteroid 2309, that is named Mr. Spock for the famous character in the television series, Star Trek.

Many asteroids can be observed through telescopes. In good viewing conditions, Vesta, the fourth-largest asteroid at 501 km in diameter, is visible even to the naked eye. From the Earth, most asteroids appear as tiny points of light. To be studied in detail, an asteroid must either come relatively close to the Earth or be visited by a space probe.

The Asteroid Belt
The Asteroid Belt lies between the orbits of the planets Mars and Jupiter. Asteroids are lumps of rock or metal that were left over after the Solar System formed. They vary greatly in size – from 933 kilometres to only a few centimetres in diameter. Occasionally, some fragments of asteroids survive the passage through the Earth’s atmosphere and reach the Earth’s surface. They are called meteorites.
