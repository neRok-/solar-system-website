The Earth

THE EARTH is one of nine planets that travel around the Sun in the Solar System. Of these planets, the Earth is the third nearest to the Sun. It is the only planet known to support life. Almost 5 billion years ago, within a disc of material spinning around the Sun, a cloud of gas and dust began to condense into a solid mass – the young Earth. At first, this mass was very cold, but later it was melted as a result of the heat generated by radioactivity. Heavy metals collected at the centre of the Earth and rocks floated near the surface. After millions of years, the rocks formed a hard crust and the oceans and the atmosphere appeared.

Earth’s origins

The Earth developed from a spinning cloud of gas and dust nearly 5 billion years ago.

The Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the largest of the four inner planets. The Earth is unique in the Solar System, having abundant supplies of water, oxygen, and nitrogen, all of which are essential to life as we know it. Water covers about 70 per cent of the Earth’s surface.

Composition

The Earth’s internal structure is similar to that of the other inner planets, consisting of a solid metal inner core of iron and nickel, about 1,200 km in radius, surrounded by a molten metal outer core, about 2,300 km thick, followed by layers of molten, semimolten, and solid rock. Conditions at the surface, however, are very different: no other planet has liquid water or an oxygen-rich atmosphere, and no other planet is known to support life.

Atmosphere

The Earth’s atmosphere is made up of 77 per cent nitrogen, 22 per cent oxygen, and small amounts of argon, carbon dioxide, water vapour, and other gases. The atmosphere acts as a protective layer, blocking harmful rays from the Sun. The proportion of gases in the atmosphere are slowly changing due to human influence.

The Magnetosphere

The Earth is surrounded by an enormous magnetic field, called the magnetosphere. As the Earth spins, its iron core generates electric currents that extend into space, forming this “blanket” around the planet. The magnetosphere protects the Earth from the solar wind (high-speed streams of charged particles that flow from the Sun). Some of the particles are trapped by the magnetosphere, forming two regions called the Van Allen belts.
