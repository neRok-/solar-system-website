The Sun

 

The sun is a 5-billion year old main sequence star. It is a sphere composed mainly of hydrogen and helium gases, about 1.4 million kilometres in diameter. Four distinct layers surround the sun’s core. These are the radiation zone, the convection zone, the photosphere, and the chromosphere. In the core, where temperatures reach 15 million °C, nuclear reactions convert mass into electromagnetic radiation, a form of energy. The radiation takes 2 million years to travel from the Sun’s core to the photosphere, where it is emitted into space in the form of light, infrared radiation (heat), ultraviolet radiation, and other forms of energy.

 

The Sun is an unremarkable star situated close to the edge of one of the arms of our galaxy called the Orion arm. It looks so bright simply because it is so near to us, appearing 400,000 times brighter than the full Moon. Its diameter is 1.4 million km, more than 100 times the diameter of the Earth. The Sun has seven times the mass of the average star and contains 750 times the mass of all its planets put together.

The Sun is an entirely gaseous body. Approximately 60 per cent of its mass is in the form of hydrogen gas, and the rest is mainly helium gas. In the Sun’s core, hydrogen nuclei fuse to form helium nuclei by nuclear fusion. In the process 0.7 per cent of the mass being fused is converted into energy. Of the 600 million tonnes of hydrogen that fuse in the Sun’s core each second, 4 million tonnes are converted to energy. It has been calculated that the Sun’s hydrogen fuel will last for another 5 billion years.

The energy produced in the Sun’s core travels outwards through a layer called the radiation zone by a process of continous absorption and re-emission by the dense gases that form this layer. Within the convection zone, which surrounds the radiation zone, the energy flows outwards through a different mechanism. Masses of gas rise to the surface, radiate away both heat and light, and then cool and sink back again.

The visible surface of the Sun, called the photosphere, has a temperature of about 6,000°C and is only a few hundred kilometres thick. Because most of the Sun’s light output that reaches us comes from the photosphere, the Sun appears to have a relatively sharp edge. In reality there is no definite surface to the Sun, but rather a gradual fading away of the gaseous matter into space. Surrounding the photosphere is the chromosphere, a layer of reddish-pink gas, and the corona, which only becomes visible during a total solar eclipses.

The Sun rotates about its own axis at different rates depending on latitude. This rotation creates a magnetic field. Because the Sun’s equatorial region spins faster than its polar regions, the magnetic field lines become wound up inside the Sun. If they break through the photosphere, the magnetic lines cause solar activity such as sunspots, flares, and prominences. This activity, particularly that of the sunspots, tends to follow an 11-year cycle.

Sunspots are relatively cool regions on the Sun’s surface, caused by magnetic fields that prevent hot material reaching the surface from inside. The central part of a sunspot, the umbra, has a temperature of about 4,200°C, which is about 1,500°C cooler than the surrounding solar photosphere. Although the sunspots are glowing with heat, they appear dark against their hotter, brighter surroundings.

Violent solar activity often occurs near sunspots. Solar flares are “flashes” of energy that may last for several hours. They occur when a massive build up of magnetic energy is suddenly released. Some of them cause disturbances in the Earth’s upper atmosphere. Prominences are eruptions of flaming gas that may shoot hundreds of thousands of kilometres into space. Looped prominences may remain suspended by the solar magnetic field for weeks at a time.

The outermost layer of the Sun’s atmosphere, the corona, is not static but is continually expanding and giving off a stream of electrically charged particles into space. This material is called the solar wind and consists of protons, electrons, and ions (electrically charged atoms). This material is ejected from the Sun at average speeds of about 450 kilometres per second and bathes the whole of the Solar System. Among other effects, the solar wind distorts the Earth’s magnetic field and is responsible for the gas tails of comets as they approach the Sun.

It has been calculated that the Sun loses about 1.1 million tonnes of hydrogen into the solar wind every second. It would take about 100,000 billion years for the solar wind to disperse the entire mass of the Sun into interplanetary space. However, the Sun’s natural life span is only about 10 billion years.

Although the Sun was formed around 5 billion years ago, it probably reached its present temperature and brightness only about 500 million years ago. No great change is expected to occur in its size and brightness for at least 1.45 billion years. In about 5 billion years’ time, most of the hydrogen in the Sun's core will have fused to form helium. Gravity will then make the core contract, which will increase its pressure and temperature. Hydrogen will start to “burn” in a shell around the core. The new energy generated by nuclear fusion in the shell will cause the Sun's outer layers to swell until the star becomes a huge red giant. Its diameter will increase by at least 150 times. The expanded gases will fade from hot yellow to cooler red. The Sun’s brightness will increase by about 1,000 times. It will do this because the surface area will radiate more light. Its outer layers will then drift off into space to form a planetary nebula. The core will remain behind as a fading white-dwarf star.
